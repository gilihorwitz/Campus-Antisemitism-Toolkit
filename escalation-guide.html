Escalation Guide

Sometimes campus administration doesn't respond adequately, or incidents require action beyond the university level. This guide helps you know when and how to escalate.

When to Escalate

Consider escalating when:

  • You've reported an incident and received no response after 5-7 business days
  • The university's response is inadequate or dismissive
  • The incident involves criminal activity (threats, violence, vandalism)
  • There's a pattern of incidents that aren't being addressed
  • You feel unsafe and campus isn't taking appropriate action
  • The incident involves discrimination that may violate Title VI
  • You've exhausted campus channels without resolution

Escalation Levels

Level 1: Within University (Higher Administration)

If your initial contact doesn't respond or isn't helpful:

  • Go up the chain: Contact the supervisor of the person you initially reached out to
  • Contact multiple offices: Dean of Students, Title IX, Diversity Office, President's Office
  • CC multiple people: Send emails with multiple administrators copied
  • Request meetings: Ask for in-person meetings with higher-level administrators

Who to contact: Dean of Students, Vice President of Student Affairs, University President, Board of Trustees

Level 2: External Advocacy Organizations

These organizations can provide support, advocacy, and sometimes legal assistance:

  • ADL (Anti-Defamation League): Reports incidents, provides advocacy, can engage with universities
  • Hillel International: Can advocate for Jewish students and engage with university leadership
  • StandWithUs: Provides educational resources and advocacy
  • AMCHA Initiative: Monitors and responds to antisemitism on campus
  • Jewish on Campus: Amplifies student voices and advocates for change

When to contact: When you need external pressure, legal support, or when campus isn't responding

Level 3: Legal and Government

For serious incidents or when discrimination may be occurring:

  • Department of Education Office for Civil Rights: File a Title VI complaint for discrimination
  • ADL Legal Support: Legal resources and support for Jewish students
  • Local police: For criminal activity (threats, violence, vandalism)
  • State Attorney General: Some states have offices that handle discrimination complaints

When to contact: When there's potential legal violation, criminal activity, or when other channels have failed

Level 4: Media and Public Pressure

Use carefully and strategically:

  • Student newspaper: Can raise awareness and pressure administration
  • Local media: Can bring public attention to issues
  • Social media: Can amplify your voice (but be thoughtful about privacy and safety)
  • National Jewish media: Forward, Jewish Journal, etc.

When to use: When other methods haven't worked, or when you want to raise broader awareness

Considerations: This can escalate situations quickly. Consider privacy, safety, and whether you're ready for public attention.

How to Escalate Effectively

Document Everything

  • Keep records of all communications
  • Document responses (or lack thereof)
  • Note dates, times, and who you've contacted
  • Save all emails and responses

Be Clear About What You've Done

When escalating, explain:

  • What incident(s) occurred
  • Who you've already contacted
  • What responses you've received (or lack thereof)
  • Why you're escalating now
  • What you're asking for

Provide Complete Information

  • Include your incident documentation
  • Provide evidence (photos, screenshots, etc.)
  • Share timeline of events
  • Explain the impact

Escalation Email Template

Escalation Email

Subject: Escalation: Unresolved Antisemitic Incident - [DATE] Dear [HIGHER-LEVEL ADMINISTRATOR], I am writing to escalate a serious matter that has not been adequately addressed through initial reporting channels. INCIDENT SUMMARY: [Brief summary of what happened, when, where] PREVIOUS ATTEMPTS TO RESOLVE: - [DATE]: Reported to [OFFICE/PERSON] - [DATE]: Follow-up email sent - [DATE]: Meeting with [PERSON] - [RESPONSE RECEIVED OR LACK THEREOF] WHY I'M ESCALATING: [Explain why the response has been inadequate - e.g., "No response received," "Response was dismissive," "No action taken," "Pattern of incidents not addressed"] IMPACT: [Describe how this affects you and other Jewish students] WHAT I'M REQUESTING: - [Specific action 1] - [Specific action 2] - [Timeline for response] I have attached my complete incident documentation and evidence. I am prepared to discuss this matter further and am available to meet at your convenience. I hope we can resolve this matter promptly. If this cannot be resolved at the university level, I will need to consider additional options including [mention relevant options - e.g., "filing a Title VI complaint," "contacting external advocacy organizations"]. Thank you for your attention to this serious matter. Best regards, [YOUR NAME] [YOUR EMAIL] [YOUR PHONE NUMBER] ATTACHMENTS: - Incident Documentation - Evidence (photos, screenshots, etc.) - Previous correspondence

Important Considerations

Timing

  • Give initial channels reasonable time to respond (5-7 business days)
  • Don't escalate too quickly, but don't wait indefinitely
  • Escalate when you have clear evidence that initial channels aren't working

Privacy and Safety

  • Consider your safety when deciding to escalate
  • Be thoughtful about what information you share publicly
  • Protect the privacy of other students unless they consent
  • Consider whether escalation might make you a target

Support

  • Don't escalate alone—get support from friends, family, or organizations
  • Consider working with advocacy organizations who have experience
  • Take care of your mental health—this can be stressful
  • Know that escalating is sometimes necessary and you have the right to do it

You're Not Alone

Escalating can feel scary, but remember:

  • You have a right to feel safe on campus
  • You have a right to have incidents taken seriously
  • There are organizations and people who want to help
  • You're doing something important for yourself and your community
  • Many students have successfully escalated and seen positive outcomes
