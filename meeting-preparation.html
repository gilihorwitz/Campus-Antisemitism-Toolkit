Meeting Preparation Framework

Meeting with administrators can feel intimidating. This framework helps you prepare, stay focused, and advocate effectively for yourself and your community.

Pre-Meeting Checklist

PRE-MEETING CHECKLIST □ Review your incident documentation □ Prepare a 1-page summary of the incident(s) □ Gather all evidence (photos, screenshots, etc.) □ Identify your key talking points (3-5 main points) □ Decide what outcomes you want from the meeting □ Research who you're meeting with (their role, responsibilities) □ Prepare questions you want answered □ Consider bringing a support person (friend, Hillel staff, etc.) □ Bring a notebook to take notes □ Set a reminder to follow up after the meeting

Meeting Agenda Template

MEETING AGENDA Date: [DATE] Time: [TIME] Location: [LOCATION] Attendees: [LIST NAMES AND TITLES] AGENDA ITEMS: 1. Introduction & Context (5 minutes) - Brief overview of why we're meeting - Your goals for the conversation 2. Incident Summary (10 minutes) - Present the facts - Share impact on you/community - Show evidence if relevant 3. Discussion (15 minutes) - University's response so far - What policies/procedures apply - What actions will be taken 4. Questions & Next Steps (10 minutes) - Your questions - Timeline for follow-up - How you'll be kept informed 5. Closing (5 minutes) - Summary of agreements - Confirmation of next steps - Thank you

Talking Points Framework

TALKING POINTS FRAMEWORK OPENING STATEMENT: "I'm here today because [brief reason]. I want to work with the university to address this issue and ensure Jewish students feel safe and supported on campus." KEY POINTS TO MAKE: 1. THE INCIDENT(S) - What happened (factual, brief) - When and where - Impact on you/others 2. THE PATTERN (if applicable) - Is this part of a larger pattern? - Other incidents you're aware of - Escalating nature? 3. YOUR CONCERNS - Safety concerns - Academic impact - Community impact - Long-term implications 4. WHAT YOU'RE ASKING FOR - Specific actions you want to see - Policy changes or enforcement - Support resources - Communication/transparency 5. WHY IT MATTERS - Impact on Jewish students - University's responsibility - Legal/Title VI considerations - University's values/mission CLOSING STATEMENT: "I appreciate you taking the time to meet. I'm committed to working together to address this, and I look forward to hearing about next steps."

During the Meeting

  • Take notes: Write down what's said, who said it, and any commitments made.
  • Stay focused: Don't let the conversation get derailed. Refer back to your agenda if needed.
  • Ask for specifics: If they say "we'll look into it," ask for timelines and next steps.
  • Don't accept blame: You're not responsible for the antisemitism. Don't let anyone suggest otherwise.
  • Bring it back to impact: If they minimize the incident, redirect to how it affected you.
  • Ask about policies: What policies apply? What's the process? What are your rights?
  • Request documentation: Ask for a summary email or written response.

Questions to Ask

  • "What is the university's policy on [relevant topic]?"
  • "What steps will be taken to address this incident?"
  • "What is the timeline for these actions?"
  • "How will I be kept informed of progress?"
  • "What support resources are available for me?"
  • "What can be done to prevent similar incidents?"
  • "Who else should be involved in addressing this?"
  • "What are my options if I'm not satisfied with the response?"

After the Meeting

  • Send a summary email: Within 24 hours, email a summary of what was discussed and agreed upon.
  • Follow up: If they committed to actions, follow up on the timeline they gave.
  • Document everything: Add meeting notes to your incident documentation.
  • Share appropriately: Update trusted friends/family, but be thoughtful about what you share publicly.
  • Know your next steps: If the response isn't adequate, know your escalation options (see Escalation Guide).

If You're Nervous

It's completely normal to feel anxious about these meetings. Remember:

  • You have a right to feel safe on campus
  • You're not asking for special treatment—you're asking for equal treatment
  • Bringing a support person is totally okay
  • You can take breaks if you need to
  • You can follow up in writing if you forget to say something
  • You're doing something important for yourself and your community
